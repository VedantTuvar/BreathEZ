Air Quality Index Guide

Understanding AQI levels and their health implications

AQI Scale & Health Recommendations

0–50 | Good

Health Implications: Air quality is considered satisfactory.

Cautionary Statement: Air pollution poses little or no risk.

51–100 | Moderate

Health Implications: Air quality is acceptable for most people.

Cautionary Statement: Unusually sensitive people should consider reducing prolonged outdoor exertion.

101–150 | Unhealthy for Sensitive Groups

Health Implications: Members of sensitive groups may experience health effects.

Cautionary Statement: Children, elderly, and people with respiratory conditions should limit outdoor exertion.

151–200| Unhealthy

Health Implications: Everyone may begin to experience health effects.

Cautionary Statement: Everyone should limit outdoor exertion.

201–300 | Very Unhealthy

Health Implications: Health warnings of emergency conditions.

Cautionary Statement: Sensitive groups should remain indoors.

301–500 | Hazardous

Health Implications: Health alert: everyone may experience serious health effects.

Cautionary Statement: Everyone should avoid outdoor activities.

Key Pollutants

PM2.5

Particles: Smaller than 2.5 micrometers

Sources: Vehicle exhaust, industrial emissions, wood burning

Health Effects: Respiratory and cardiovascular issues

PM10

Particles: Smaller than 10 micrometers

Sources: Dust, pollen, vehicle emissions

Health Effects: Respiratory irritation, reduced lung function

O₃

Type: Ground-level ozone

Sources: Chemical reactions between NOx and VOCs in sunlight

Health Effects: Respiratory inflammation, chest pain

NO₂

Type: Nitrogen dioxide

Sources: Vehicle emissions, power plants

Health Effects: Respiratory symptoms, reduced immunity

Sensitive Groups

Children

Developing respiratory systems make children more vulnerable to air pollution.

Older Adults (65+)

Age-related decline in immune function increases vulnerability.

People with Conditions

Heart disease, asthma, COPD, and diabetes increase sensitivity.
